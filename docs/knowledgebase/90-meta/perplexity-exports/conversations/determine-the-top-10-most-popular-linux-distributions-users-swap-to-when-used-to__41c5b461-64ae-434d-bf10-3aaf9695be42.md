## Assistant

For users transitioning from Windows to Linux, especially those who prioritize development for cross-platform GUI apps and gaming, it’s essential to find a Linux distribution that offers both ease of use and long-term satisfaction. Based on your preferences (favoring package managers like Nix and YUM over APT), a focus on gaming, and the importance of a good desktop environment (DE), here are the top Linux distributions that users typically switch to from Windows and tend to stick with:

## **Top 5 Linux Distros for Windows Users Who Stick With Linux**

### **1. Pop!_OS**
- **Why it stands out**: Developed by System76, Pop!_OS is tailored for developers and gamers, with built-in support for NVIDIA and AMD GPUs. It is based on Ubuntu but offers a more streamlined experience with a clean interface and tiling window management for productivity.
- **Desktop Environment**: GNOME (with customizations by System76).
- **Package Management**: APT (Ubuntu-based), but users can integrate Flatpak or Snap.
- **Gaming**: Excellent for gaming, with pre-installed drivers for NVIDIA cards.
- **Why users stick with it**: Pop!_OS is known for its ease of use, stability, and excellent hardware support, making it a popular choice among gamers and developers alike.[3][7]

### **2. Fedora**
- **Why it stands out**: Fedora is known for being cutting-edge, offering the latest software and technologies. It’s backed by Red Hat and has strong ties to enterprise environments. Fedora’s focus on open-source principles makes it ideal for developers.
- **Desktop Environment**: GNOME by default, but Fedora Spins offers KDE Plasma, XFCE, Cinnamon, and others.
- **Package Management**: DNF/YUM (RPM-based).
- **Gaming**: While not as gaming-centric as Pop!_OS, Fedora’s frequent updates mean it supports the latest hardware and software innovations.
- **Why users stick with it**: Developers love Fedora for its stability combined with access to the latest tools. Its robust security features also make it appealing.[2][8]

### **3. Manjaro**
- **Why it stands out**: Manjaro is based on Arch Linux but simplifies the installation process while retaining Arch’s flexibility. It offers a rolling release model, ensuring users always have the latest software without needing to reinstall.
- **Desktop Environment**: Available with KDE Plasma, XFCE, GNOME, and others.
- **Package Management**: Pacman (Arch-based), with access to the Arch User Repository (AUR) for additional software.
- **Gaming**: Manjaro is well-suited for gaming due to its rolling release model and access to cutting-edge software from the AUR.
- **Why users stick with it**: Manjaro provides a balance between cutting-edge features and stability. Its flexibility appeals to power users who want control without the complexity of pure Arch.[6][7]

### **4. Linux Mint**
- **Why it stands out**: Linux Mint is one of the most popular distributions for users switching from Windows due to its familiar interface. It’s based on Ubuntu but focuses on simplicity and ease of use.
- **Desktop Environment**: Cinnamon (most Windows-like), MATE, or XFCE.
- **Package Management**: APT (Ubuntu-based), though Flatpak is integrated for easier app installation.
- **Gaming**: Decent support for gaming via Steam and Proton; however, it lacks the pre-installed GPU drivers found in Pop!_OS.
- **Why users stick with it**: Mint’s stability, user-friendly interface, and low learning curve make it ideal for those who want a hassle-free experience.[1][14]

### **5. Zorin OS**
- **Why it stands out**: Zorin OS is designed specifically to mimic the Windows interface, making it an easy transition for new Linux users. It also offers a variety of layouts that can resemble Windows 7 or 11.
- **Desktop Environment**: Zorin Desktop (based on GNOME).
- **Package Management**: APT (Ubuntu-based).
- **Gaming**: Zorin OS supports gaming well enough but doesn’t come pre-configured like Pop!_OS or Manjaro.
- **Why users stick with it**: Zorin OS provides an incredibly smooth transition from Windows due to its familiar interface. The learning curve is minimal, making it a favorite among non-tech-savvy users.[14][1]

## **Honorable Mentions**

### **6. openSUSE**
- Known for its powerful YaST configuration tool and excellent documentation. Offers both stable (Leap) and rolling release (Tumbleweed) versions. It uses Zypper as its package manager (RPM-based), which may appeal to those who prefer YUM-like systems.[5][2]
  
### **7. Solus**
- Solus is an independent distro known for its sleek Budgie desktop environment. It’s designed with simplicity in mind but has strong multimedia support out of the box.[1]

## **Conclusion**
For cross-platform GUI development and gaming while avoiding apt-get package management:

1. If you prioritize gaming *and* development tools, go with ***Pop!_OS*** or ***Manjaro***.
2. If you want cutting-edge software without sacrificing stability or prefer YUM/DNF-like package management systems, ***Fedora*** is your best bet.
3. For a more traditional desktop experience similar to Windows, ***Linux Mint*** or ***Zorin OS*** will provide an easy transition.

These distributions are widely praised by users who have switched from Windows and tend to stick with them due to their stability, ease of use, and strong community support.

If you're looking for a Linux distribution closest to the Steam Deck's SteamOS, here are some top contenders based on your preferences for development, gaming, and package management:

### **1. ChimeraOS**
- **Closest to SteamOS**: ChimeraOS is explicitly designed to replicate the Steam Deck experience on other hardware. It’s based on Arch Linux, just like SteamOS, and comes with a streamlined interface that resembles the Steam Deck’s Game Mode. It’s optimized for gaming and supports various game stores (Steam, GOG, Epic Games) out of the box.
- **Why it stands out**: ChimeraOS offers a seamless gaming experience with minimal setup, making it ideal for users who want a console-like experience on their PC or handheld device. It also includes built-in support for emulation and game tweaks.
- **Package Management**: Pacman (Arch-based), with access to the Arch User Repository (AUR) for additional software.
- **Why users stick with it**: The ease of use combined with its focus on gaming makes it an excellent choice for gamers who want a Steam Deck-like experience without the complexity of setting up a traditional Linux desktop.[1][2][4]

### **2. Nobara**
- **Fedora-based Alternative**: Nobara is a custom version of Fedora Linux built by GloriousEggroll (the creator of Proton-GE). It incorporates numerous gaming optimizations and bleeding-edge software like Gamescope and Mesa, making it one of the best distros for gaming performance.
- **Why it stands out**: Nobara is designed to reduce friction for gamers by pre-configuring many settings and optimizations. It's Fedora-based, which aligns with your preference for YUM/DNF package management over APT.
- **Package Management**: DNF/YUM (RPM-based).
- **Why users stick with it**: Nobara is highly stable while providing cutting-edge gaming performance. It's also flexible enough for development work, making it a great all-around choice.[3][5][1]

### **3. Bazzite**
- **Fedora Atomic Alternative**: Bazzite is another Fedora-based distro but built on Fedora Kinoite/Silverblue principles (immutable filesystem). It’s tailored for gaming and offers a similar experience to ChimeraOS but with Fedora’s stability and package management.
- **Why it stands out**: Bazzite is ideal if you prefer an immutable filesystem (where system files are locked down for stability) but still want flexibility in gaming performance.
- **Package Management**: DNF/YUM (RPM-based).
- **Why users stick with it**: Bazzite offers a balance between Fedora’s stability and cutting-edge gaming features, making it appealing to those who want both development tools and a hassle-free gaming setup.[5][1]

### **Conclusion**
For the closest experience to the Steam Deck:
- ***ChimeraOS*** is your best bet if you want something nearly identical to SteamOS but with more flexibility in terms of hardware support.
  
If you prefer Fedora's package management:
- ***Nobara*** or ***Bazzite*** would be excellent alternatives, especially if you're looking for something more stable or customizable while still being optimized for gaming.

All three options offer strong support for gaming and development, but ChimeraOS will give you the closest feel to using a Steam Deck on other hardware.
