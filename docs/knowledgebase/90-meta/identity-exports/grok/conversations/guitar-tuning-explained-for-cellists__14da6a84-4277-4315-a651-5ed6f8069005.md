**Standard guitar tuning is E-A-D-G-B-E (from lowest/thickest string to highest/thinnest).**<grok:render card_id="e59325" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation"><argument name="citation_id">1</argument></grok:render>

As a cellist, you are accustomed to perfect fifths between strings (C-G-D-A for the standard cello). Guitar tuning primarily uses **perfect fourths** (five semitones) between most adjacent strings, with one exception: a major third (four semitones) between the G and B strings. This creates the "kink" you notice.<grok:render card_id="28dd5e" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation"><argument name="citation_id">1</argument></grok:render>

### Why This Tuning?
- **Historical and practical origins**: It evolved from earlier instruments like the lute and baroque guitar (often five strings tuned A-D-G-B-E). Adding the low E completed the modern setup. The goal was a balance of musical convenience and physical comfort for the hand.<grok:render card_id="6ce50b" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation"><argument name="citation_id">9</argument></grok:render>
- **Chord playability**: The mostly fourths tuning, with the G-to-B major third, makes common open chords (especially in keys like C, G, D, A, E) easy to finger with minimal hand movement. It allows efficient shapes for triads and power chords.
- **Scale and melody access**: It facilitates playing scales and melodies across strings with reasonable finger stretches and access to open strings for resonance in popular keys.<grok:render card_id="c9c567" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation"><argument name="citation_id">1</argument></grok:render>
- **Even (all fourths or all fifths) tuning is avoided** because it complicates common chord voicings. All fourths (e.g., E-A-D-G-C-F) would require more stretches or awkward fingerings for many standard chords. The current system optimizes for the average hand size and Western harmonic traditions, prioritizing harmony over perfect symmetry.<grok:render card_id="32914a" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation"><argument name="citation_id">8</argument></grok:render>

This compromise has endured for centuries because it works well for most players, especially in folk, rock, and pop contexts relevant to songwriting.

### Playing a Basic Scale (e.g., C Major)
As a cellist, you already know the C major scale pattern: **whole-whole-half-whole-whole-whole-half** steps (W-W-H-W-W-W-H).

On guitar, start simply on one string to map the fretboard (frets are semitones):
- Open low E string: E
- 1st fret: F
- 2nd: F#
- 3rd: G, etc.

**C major example on the A string** (third fret is C):
- 3rd fret (C) → 5th (D, W) → 7th (E, W) → 8th (F, H) → 10th (G, W) → 12th (A, W) → 14th (B, W) → 15th (C, H).

Use one finger per fret (index for 1st fret position, etc.) for efficiency. Practice ascending and descending.

For full fretboard scales, learn **CAGED positions** or basic patterns. Start with open-position C major (using open strings) or the pentatonic scale for simpler melody writing.<grok:render card_id="e83241" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation"><argument name="citation_id">23</argument></grok:render>
