## Japan's Trade Before 1930: Starting Point

Japan shifted from isolation under the Tokugawa shogunate's sakoku policy, which limited foreign contact to Dutch and Chinese traders at Nagasaki for over 200 years. This kept the economy self-reliant but technologically behind. The Meiji Restoration in 1868 ended that, forcing open ports via unequal treaties with Western powers like the US and Britain. Trade was created to catch up industrially—importing resources and tech Japan lacked, solving stagnation from resource scarcity and no global markets, unlike pre-Meiji reliance on domestic agriculture and crafts that couldn't scale for modernization.

## Key Periods Covered

Focus on 1900-1929, building from late 1800s data. Trade grew from 20 million yen total in 1868 to over 1 billion yen by 1912, then higher in 1920s. Balance often unfavorable (more imports), seen as investment for growth, not weakness.

## What Japan Requested (Imports)

Japan imported to fuel industrialization—raw materials it lacked (islands have limited minerals/ag land), foodstuffs for growing population, machinery for factories. Compared to pre-Meiji self-sufficiency, this solved shortages that blocked large-scale production.

- **Raw Materials (Major Share)**: Raw cotton 32% of imports (200 million yen by 1912, from India 103M, US 55M, China 19M)—for textile industry. Wool 9% (13M yen, Australia/GB). Iron ores 9% (from China). Flax/hemp/jute 7M yen (Philippines/China). Indigo 2M yen (Germany).

- **Foodstuffs and Fertilizers (114M yen in 1912)**: Rice 29M (India/Indo-China/Siam) for crop failures. Beans 8M (China). Wheat 1.5M (US). Sugar 15M (Dutch Indies/Philippines). Fertilizers 50M (oil cakes from India/China, chemicals from US/Germany)—to boost agriculture, solving soil depletion from intensive farming.

- **Metals and Machinery (120M yen)**: Iron/steel 60M (GB 12.5M, Germany 10M, US 9.5M)—for shipbuilding/railways. Petroleum 13M (Dutch Indies 4M, US 9M). Machinery 4% overall (boilers/engines from GB, rolling stock from Germany/US).

- **Other**: Soybeans 3%, cereals from China (pre-1914). Luxuries post-1896 gold standard adoption, dropping prices.

## What Japan Provided (Exports)

Japan exported to pay for imports—starting with agriculture, shifting to manufactures as factories grew. This solved pre-Meiji export limits (mostly tea/silk via middlemen), creating direct markets and currency inflows.

- **Agricultural/Raw (210M yen in 1912)**: Raw silk 15-160M (two-thirds of exports, 200M by 1912, 80% world share, to US). Tea 14M (to US, volume down but value up). Forestry/camphor 3M (to Europe).

- **Manufactures (200M yen)**: Cotton yarns 54M, knit goods 7M, towels 2M (to Orientals). Silk tissues 27M, handkerchiefs 4M. Porcelain 5.5M. Straw braids 17M, mats 4M. Matches 12M. Wool articles 19%, rayon 15% in 1920s. Machinery 3% by late 1920s, rising in high-skilled to colonies.

- **Mine/Marine (110M yen)**: Coal 20M (3M tons, to China). Copper 25M (to China). Fish/seaweeds 10M.

- **Industrial Focus (Interwar Early)**: Textiles dominant (65-70% low-end like yarns/piece goods), to Southeast Asia/US. High-skilled (machinery/steel) to colonies for their development.

## How Trade Happened

Sea routes—Japan built a merchant fleet (e.g., NYK line) post-Meiji, solving isolation by controlling shipping, unlike pre-1850s no navy. Ports like Yokohama/Kobe handled global traffic. Despite wars (e.g., 1904-05 with Russia), trade with non-belligerents continued; post-war, controlled Korea/Taiwan supplied nearby resources, reducing "buttfuck nowhere" reliance. Distant partners via Pacific/Indian Ocean routes, thousands of miles but routine by steamships. Wars disrupted specifics (e.g., less Russian trade), but overall volume grew—e.g., exports doubled 1900-1913. No total isolation; diplomacy/treaties ensured access. Worth noting: This setup worked until 1930s aggression strained relations, but pre-1930, it enabled rapid growth.
