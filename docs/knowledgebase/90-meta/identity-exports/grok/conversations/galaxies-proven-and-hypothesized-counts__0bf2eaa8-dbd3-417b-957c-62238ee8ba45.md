## Hypothesized Total in Entire Universe

The entire universe extends beyond what light reaches us. Cosmology models like inflation solved horizon and flatness problems in big bang theory by making space expand rapidly early on.

Size is unknown—could be finite but huge, or infinite.<grok:render card_id="ec7396" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation">

If finite, at least way more than observable; some models suggest volume hundreds of times larger, but no firm count.

No point chasing exacts here—it's speculative. Worth time if you're into cosmology, but alternatives like multiverse ideas don't change the infinity possibility.

## Proven Galaxies

Start from galaxy surveys, which built on early telescope observations like Hubble's to catalog distant objects beyond the Milky Way.

These surveys solved the problem of manually spotting galaxies one by one, which was limited to thousands before automated digital imaging and spectroscopy came along.

As of recent data, around 200 million galaxies have been cataloged and confirmed through major surveys like SDSS.<grok:render card_id="c5e0ec" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation">
<argument name="citation_id">57</argument>
</grok:render>

This number covers photometric identifications, where shape and color confirm they're galaxies.

Spectroscopic confirmations, which measure redshifts for distance proof, are lower at several million.

Newer surveys like Euclid add tens of millions more, but overlaps exist with older catalogs.

## Hypothesized Galaxies

Estimates come from deep-field images, like Hubble's Ultra Deep Field, extrapolated across the entire observable universe.

This method addressed undercounting faint, distant galaxies that older telescopes missed due to light limits.

Common figure is about 2 trillion galaxies total in the observable universe.<grok:render card_id="aa81e9" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation">
