### The Muhlenberg Legend Explained
The legend typically claims that Frederick Augustus Muhlenberg, the first Speaker of the U.S. House of Representatives (and of German descent), cast a decisive tie-breaking vote against making German the official language. Versions place this in 1774 (Continental Congress), 1794–1795 (U.S. House), or even as a Pennsylvania state matter. Muhlenberg is portrayed as "betraying" his heritage, supposedly saying something like "the faster the Germans become Americans, the better it will be."

### A Detailed Examination of the "One Vote" Language Myth in American History

The conversation captures a common modern retelling of one of America's most enduring linguistic urban legends: the idea that the young United States came within a single vote of adopting a non-English official language. Initially framed around French (tied to Revolutionary War alliances), the speaker quickly corrects it to German upon checking—accurately identifying the core of the story while noting its legendary status. This section provides a comprehensive breakdown, drawing on primary historical analysis, scholarly debunkings, and the legend's cultural evolution.

#### Historical Context: Language in the Early United States
The U.S. has never enacted a federal official language, a deliberate choice rooted in the Founding Fathers' avoidance of linguistic mandates (unlike many European nations). English dominated de facto due to its prevalence among settlers and leaders, but significant German-speaking populations—especially in Pennsylvania, Virginia, and Maryland—numbered in the hundreds of thousands by the 1790s, comprising up to one-third of Pennsylvania's residents in some estimates. These "Pennsylvania Dutch" (from Deutsch) communities prompted occasional calls for German-language accommodations, but never for replacing English entirely.

French influence was cultural and diplomatic (e.g., the 1778 Treaty of Alliance was bilingual), but no serious congressional push existed to make French official. Proposals for other languages, like Hebrew or Greek, appear only in satirical or apocryphal stories (e.g., jokes attributing Greek to admiration for classical republics or Hebrew to biblical ties).

#### The Real 1794–1795 Petition and Vote
The kernel of truth lies in a petition submitted to the Third Congress (1793–1795) by German-Americans in Virginia, requesting translations of federal statutes into German under the Naturalization Act's provisions for immigrant integration. The House committee recommended rejection, citing cost and precedent.

Debate occurred on January 13, 1795. Rather than voting directly on translations, the House considered a motion to adjourn and revisit the issue—effectively tabling it. This procedural motion failed narrowly:
- Sources converge on a 42–41 defeat or a tie broken routinely (Muhlenberg did not dramatically intervene as Speaker for this vote).
- The petition was formally rejected later without recorded drama.

Historians emphasize: This was about accessibility for non-English speakers, not elevating German to official status. #### Origins and Propagation of the Legend
The story traces to Franz von Löher's 1847 book *Geschichte und Zustände der Deutschen in Amerika*, which criticized Muhlenberg for a supposed Pennsylvania vote (not federal). Löher, a German nationalist, framed it as cultural betrayal. By the late 19th century, it migrated to the U.S. Congress in retellings.

It gained traction in:
- Early 20th-century Ripley's Believe It or Not! features.
- 1930s German-American Bund propaganda (exaggerating German influence).
- Post-WWII "importance of voting" lists (e.g., 1987 Ann Landers column claiming "1776: one vote gave America English instead of German").
