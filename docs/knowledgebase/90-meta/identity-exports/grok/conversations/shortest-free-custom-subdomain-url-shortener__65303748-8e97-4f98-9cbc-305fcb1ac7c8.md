The part you're referring to — the **.com**, **.net**, **.org**, etc., at the end of a domain name — is called a **top-level domain** (commonly abbreviated as **TLD**).

**Key points**
- It's the highest level in the Domain Name System (DNS) hierarchy, coming right after the final dot in a domain like example.com.
- TLDs help classify websites by purpose (e.g., commercial, organizational), geography, or other categories.
- The most common ones like .com and .net are **generic top-level domains** (gTLDs), while two-letter ones like .uk or .ca are **country-code top-level domains** (ccTLDs).
- This terminology is standard across technical sources, registrars, and the organization that oversees the global DNS (ICANN).

**Examples in context**
- In **kotor.net**, the TLD is **.net** (originally meant for network-related entities, now used more generally).
- In **google.com**, the TLD is **.com** (short for commercial, but now the default for most sites).
- In **wikipedia.org**, the TLD is **.org** (originally for organizations, especially nonprofits).

**Why it matters**
TLDs influence branding, trust, availability, and sometimes SEO perceptions, though modern search engines treat most gTLDs similarly. People often just call them "domain extensions" or "domain suffixes" in everyday language.

---

**Detailed explanation of top-level domains (TLDs)**

A top-level domain (TLD) is the last segment of a domain name in the hierarchical Domain Name System (DNS) of the Internet, appearing immediately after the final dot. For instance, in a fully qualified domain name like www.example.com, the TLD is .com. TLDs sit at the highest level below the invisible root zone and are managed globally under the coordination of ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers) through its IANA functions.

The DNS structure breaks down like this:
- Root (invisible)
- TLD (.com, .net, etc.)
- Second-level domain (example in example.com)
- Subdomains (www or blog in www.example.com)

**Main categories of TLDs**
TLDs fall into several broad groups, with the most relevant to everyday use being generic and country-code varieties.

| Category              | Description                                                                 | Examples                          | Typical Use Cases                                      | Notes                                                                 |

**.com and .net specifics**
- **.com** — Originally derived from "commercial" (intended for businesses); launched in 1985 and now the most popular and trusted TLD globally, with hundreds of millions registered. It's widely regarded as the default for any site.
- **.net** — Originally from "network" (meant for internet service providers and infrastructure); also launched in 1985. Today it's a common alternative when .com is unavailable, especially for tech-related projects.

**Common everyday terms**
While "top-level domain" is the precise technical term, people casually refer to it as:
- Domain extension
- Domain suffix
- TLD (the acronym is very widely used)

This is consistent across authoritative sources like Wikipedia, ICANN-related documentation, major registrars (GoDaddy, Namecheap, Network Solutions), and educational sites (Wix, Cloudflare).
