## ADHD and ADD Terminology

ADD started as a label in the 1980s for attention issues without hyperactivity, back when diagnoses split hyperactive and non-hyperactive types. It solved the problem of lumping all attention deficits together under older terms like "hyperkinetic reaction," which ignored people who weren't fidgety but still couldn't focus. Now, ADD isn't an official diagnosis—it's folded into ADHD as the "predominantly inattentive" type.<grok:render card_id="f1e59c" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation">

## Core Differences in Practice

If someone says "ADD," they usually mean inattentive ADHD: trouble sustaining focus, organizing tasks, forgetfulness, but no constant restlessness or impulsivity. ADHD adds hyperactivity like fidgeting or interrupting, or both in combined type.<grok:render card_id="9ea869" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation">
<argument name="citation_id">13</argument>
</grok:render>

This matters because treatments differ—stimulants help both, but behavioral strategies target specific symptoms. Worth your time if symptoms disrupt life broadly; otherwise, it's just a label.

## Inattentive ADHD vs. Social Media Damage

Social media shortens attention spans through constant novelty, notifications, and dopamine hits, training brains for quick switches over deep focus. It evolved from older media like TV ads, but apps like TikTok amplify it by design to keep users hooked, solving boredom but creating addiction-like habits.<grok:render card_id="03f714" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation">

## How to Spot the Real Thing

Ask: Does inattention persist offline, like forgetting keys or zoning out in conversations, predating heavy social media use? ADHD hits multiple life areas; tech effects fade with reduced scrolling.<grok:render card_id="f3d41f" card_type="citation_card" type="render_inline_citation">
