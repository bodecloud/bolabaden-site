## Controlled vs. Automatic

You mean the pair from cognitive psychology: **controlled** actions (conscious, effortful) versus **automatic** actions (unconscious, effortless).

### Controlled (Conscious Doing)
This built on early behaviorism's focus on observable habits—think Pavlov's dogs reacting without thought. But by the 1970s, researchers like Shiffrin and Schneider saw limits: simple reactions work for survival basics (spotting threats), yet complex stuff like planning a route needs deliberate focus. Controlled processing fixes that by letting you override autopilot for accuracy, though it tires you out fast. Worth your time if the task risks errors; otherwise, it's overkill.

### Automatic (Unconscious Doing)
Evolved from instinct theories—Freud's unconscious drives, but updated in dual-process models (Kahneman's System 1). Pre-1970s, we lumped all behavior as learned responses, but that ignored how practice turns effort into reflex (tying shoes). Automatic solves overload: no brainpower wasted on basics, freeing you for threats or novelty. Downsides? Biases sneak in unchecked. Better than nothing for routines, but check it against controlled when stakes rise.

Pick controlled for new/high-risk stuff; automatic for the grind. Neither's perfect—humans glitch either way.

**Volitional**

That’s the single word: doing something **volitionally** = consciously, on purpose.  
The opposite is **involuntary** (or non-volitional) = unconsciously / reflexively.

Fits your “has a v” rule and it’s the precise psych term.
