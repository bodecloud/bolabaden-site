## Why LibreOffice Exists
It forked from OpenOffice in 2010 to fix governance issues and slow updates in the original. OpenOffice lagged on Microsoft file compatibility and features; LibreOffice's community-driven model delivers faster fixes, like better .docx handling without format loss. No more paying Microsoft's subscription or dealing with proprietary lock-in.

## Core Matches to Word
- Full offline editing, no internet needed.
- Handles 90%+ of Word's tools: track changes, mail merge, macros, styles, tables, images.
- Exports/saves as .docx, .pdf—seamless for sharing with Word users.
- Bundled suite (Calc for Excel, Impress for PowerPoint) if you need more than docs.

## WordPad's Role Before Win11
WordPad filled the gap between Notepad's plain text limits and Word's overkill since Windows 95. It handled RTF formatting, basic tables, images—stuff like quick letters without .docx hassle. Solved the "too basic or too corporate" problem for casual users.

## What Happened in Win11
Starting with version 24H2 (late 2024), Microsoft axed it entirely. No updates, no install option. They split duties: Notepad for .txt (now with tabs, search highlights, but still no rich formatting). Word for .rtf/.doc—pushing their paid suite.

## Official "Replacement"?
Nothing direct. MS says use Notepad for plain stuff, Word for anything formatted. If you're on free tier, Notepad's upgrades (dark mode, autosave) cover basics, but expect RTF glitches or export pains.

## Is It Worth Bothering?
Doubtful—MS wants you hooked on 365 subs. Revive old WordPad by copying files from Win10 (easy if you have access), or grab free alternatives like LibreOffice Writer (from our last chat—handles RTF natively, no ads). Better? Test Notepad first; if it chokes on your files, switch. Why force this? Streamlining, they claim—realist take: funnel to revenue.
