## Background

2009. Height of Bush-era torture debates. Waterboarding used on detainees like Abu Zubaydah. Conservatives defended it as "enhanced interrogation," not torture. Came from military training programs like SERE, meant to resist enemy tactics, but flipped to extract info from suspects post-9/11. Solved the problem of getting confessions without visible scars—unlike old-school beatings—but created new ones: unreliable info, legal blowback, moral rot.

Muller, a conservative shock jock, backed the "not torture" camp. Wanted to shut up critics by trying it himself.

## The Setup

Live on his Chicago radio show. Listeners voted: him or co-host. He lost. Done in a dingy storage room next to studio. Table 7 feet long, legs up, feet tied. Paramedic on standby. Marine sergeant handled it, said average lasts 14 seconds.

## The Rules and Signal

Held a toy cow. Drop it to stop. Simple panic button for when words fail.

## What Happened

Strapped down. Water poured over face. Thrashed, tossed the cow instantly. Lasted 6-7 seconds. Not "a second," but your memory's fuzzy—close.

## Immediate Reaction

Sprang up gasping. Compared to childhood near-drowning. "Way worse than I thought." Admitted: "Absolutely torture." No joke, felt like actual drowning.

## Aftermath and Conclusion

Went on air right after. Said he expected to laugh it off, prove the point. Instead, flipped. "I wanted to prove it wasn't torture... I don't want to say this: absolutely torture."

Echoed journalist Christopher Hitchens' 2008 take—who also tried it, lasted seconds, concluded if that's not torture, nothing is. But Muller was the live stunt guy fitting your beads-holding bit (toy cow, whatever).

## Why This Story Matters

Proves even believers crack fast. Highlights how denial ignores biology—body thinks it's dying. Better than ignoring evidence? Ask yourself if debating "is it torture" ever solved real problems, or just excused them.

### Background
Christopher Hitchens, a journalist, wanted to test if waterboarding was torture. This came from debates after 9/11 on U.S. interrogation methods. Waterboarding started in SERE training for U.S. Special Forces—to resist enemy tactics, not use them. It solved the problem of preparing soldiers for real torture without breaking laws, unlike older methods that left marks or killed.

He did this in 2008, at 59, for a Vanity Fair article. He doubted it was torture until he tried it.

### Setup
Hitchens went to North Carolina hills. A team of ex-military, experienced in interrogations, set it up. They stayed anonymous. He signed a waiver listing risks like death or injury—ironic, since they called it "not torture."

They added safeguards: a safe word ("red") and a "dead man's handle" (beads to drop if he passed out). Unlike real captives, he could stop anytime.

He was nervous, had a doctor's note (no asthma), but hid his smoking habit. An expert warned his age made it risky—usually for fit young soldiers.

### The Experience
At a remote house, they grabbed him suddenly. Cuffed wrists to belt, black hood over head. Led him disoriented into a dark room with lights and music.

Strapped to a sloping board, head down. Legs tied. Three wet towels over his face under the hood. Water poured slowly up his nose.

He tried holding breath, but panic hit. Felt like drowning, couldn't tell inhale from exhale. Signaled stop almost immediately—lasted seconds.

They pulled him up, removed cloths. He felt ashamed, especially hearing claims like Khalid Sheikh Mohammed lasted over two minutes (doubted by handlers).

Tried again after paramedic check. Same terror, gag reflex kicked in. Would've confessed anything. Stopped quicker.

### Aftermath
Lingering effects: Woke up pushing covers off face, claustrophobic when breathless. Realized he didn't say the safe word—dropped beads unconsciously, showing false memory risk.

Handlers explained: They check for cheating (like timing breaths), add "enhancements" if needed. Called it a long time "when breathing water."

### Perspectives and Conclusions
One view from handlers: It's "necessary" against enemies, leaves no marks, subject recovers fast—better than brutal alternatives like beheading.

Opposing view (from ex-SERE instructor Malcolm Nance): It's torture, prosecuted when others do it. Risks U.S. troops, gets unreliable info, trains enemies on resistance.
