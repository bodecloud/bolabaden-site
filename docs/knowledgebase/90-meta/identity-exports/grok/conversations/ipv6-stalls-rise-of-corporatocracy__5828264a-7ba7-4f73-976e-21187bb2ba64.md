**The word you're looking for is almost certainly "corporatocracy".**

It fits the exact gap in your sentence: "That scares me. Because that means everything is controlled by the **corporatocracy**."

### Why this word clicks perfectly here
- **Definition and nuance**: Corporatocracy describes a system (economic, infrastructural, or de facto political) in which *corporations*—not governments, not a shadowy cabal of individuals, and not purely "the rich"—hold the dominant decision-making power over key aspects of society. It emphasizes *corporate institutional power* exercised through profit motives, market structure, lobbying, technical standards, and infrastructure ownership. It's distinct from "oligarchy" (which is broader and often implies rule by a small elite group of powerful *people*, whether aristocratic, political, or wealthy). You correctly rejected oligarchy because the driver here isn't a handful of mustache-twirling tycoons—it's publicly traded companies responding to ROI calculations, engineering costs, and shareholder pressure. That's textbook corporatocracy.
- **How it maps directly to CGNAT/IPv6**: Your ISP (and many others) isn't refusing IPv6 deployment out of some grand conspiracy to "avoid" it. It's a pure money-and-inertia play: deploying native IPv6 across legacy infrastructure, training staff, updating billing/monitoring systems, and replacing CGNAT bandaids costs real capital with low immediate return. Meanwhile, CGNAT lets them keep squeezing more customers onto the existing IPv4 pool. The *result*, though, is exactly what you're describing: the end-to-end principle of the original internet is broken for residential users. No more easy listening servers, no simple peer-to-peer hosting, no self-hosted anything without workarounds (VPNs, cloud relays, hole-punching services, or paid VPSes). Everything funnels through corporate-controlled choke points—your ISP's CGNAT logs, cloud providers' terms of service, app-store gatekeepers, or dedicated-server farms. That's control by *the corporations*, not "the oligarchy."

### Multiple angles on why this fits (and why other words fall short)
- **Economic/infrastructural angle**: Publicly traded ISPs optimize for quarterly earnings. IPv6 migration has massive upfront costs and uncertain short-term revenue. CGNAT is the classic "move fast and break the decentralized web" bandaid. The side effect is structural centralization: individuals lose the ability to publish/serve directly from home, while hyperscalers (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure, Cloudflare, etc.—also publicly traded) capture the traffic, the data, the monetization, and the censorship levers. Corporatocracy captures this perfectly; it's not "a few bad actors," it's the system operating as designed.
- **Freedom and precedent angle (tying in Palworld/Nintendo)**: You mentioned the Palworld lawsuit setting industry-wide precedents. Same pattern. One or two powerful players use legal/technical levers to define the boundaries of what's possible for everyone else. With CGNAT becoming normalized, the "precedent" is that consumer internet = outbound-only client, not a full peer on the network. Self-hosting, indie games with P2P, home labs, privacy tools, activist sites—all become relics or paywalled through corporate intermediaries. That's corporatocracy in action: private companies effectively writing the rules of digital participation.
- **Hanlon's Razor vs. systemic outcome**: The first speaker is right that it's mostly incompetence + profit motive, not malice. But the *outcome* is still the same: loss of user sovereignty. Corporatocracy doesn't require a conspiracy; it emerges when corporate incentives are allowed to dictate public infrastructure without sufficient counter-pressure (regulation, competition, or user revolt).
