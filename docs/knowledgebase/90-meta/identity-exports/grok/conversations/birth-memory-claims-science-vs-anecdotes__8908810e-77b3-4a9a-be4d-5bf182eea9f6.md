Has anyone ever claimed and somewhat been proven to have remembered their birth?

## Assistant

_Time: 2025-08-07T09:34:05.167Z_

There are anecdotal claims of people remembering their birth, often described as vivid sensory experiences like light, warmth, or pressure. These accounts typically come from adults under hypnosis, meditation, or spontaneous recall, sometimes tied to spiritual or psychological practices. However, no scientific evidence conclusively proves these memories are genuine. The human brain, particularly the hippocampus, responsible for forming long-term memories, is not fully developed at birth, making it unlikely for explicit, narrative memories to form. Most neuroscientists suggest these "memories" are likely constructed or influenced by later experiences, imagination, or suggestion.

Some studies, like those by psychologists such as Elizabeth Loftus, show how false memories can feel real due to suggestion or cultural narratives. Claims of birth memories often lack verifiable details and could be confabulations—blends of real and imagined events. While X posts and web anecdotes mention such experiences, they’re usually personal stories without empirical backing. For instance, a 2023 X thread discussed a user’s vivid "birth memory" of a bright light and voices, but no evidence beyond their account was provided.

Sleepwalking, or somnambulism, is a real parasomnia—a sleep disorder involving unwanted behaviors during sleep—that affects about 4% of adults and up to 17% of children, often occurring in the first few hours of sleep. In movies and TV shows, it's frequently dramatized for comedic or horror effects, showing characters wandering like zombies with arms outstretched, eyes closed, engaging in elaborate conversations, or committing bizarre acts like climbing buildings or driving cars while fully "asleep." In reality, sleepwalking episodes are typically more mundane and uncoordinated, lasting from a few seconds to 30 minutes, with the person performing routine actions like walking around the house, rearranging objects, or even eating, but without full awareness or memory afterward. Eyes are usually open (though with a blank, glassy stare), movements can be clumsy or purposeful but not zombie-like, and speech, if any, is mumbled or incoherent rather than scripted dialogue. Physiologically, sleepwalking is possible because it doesn't occur during REM (rapid eye movement) sleep, where the brain does indeed inhibit motor function through a process called REM atonia. During REM, which is when most vivid dreaming happens, the brainstem sends signals to temporarily paralyze major voluntary muscles (except for those controlling the eyes and diaphragm) to prevent you from acting out dreams—this is why you can't physically move much in REM, and disorders like REM sleep behavior disorder (where atonia fails) lead to dream enactment. Instead, sleepwalking arises during non-REM (NREM) sleep, specifically in the deep slow-wave stage (N3), when the brain is in a state of partial arousal. The mechanism involves a glitch in sleep regulation: normally, transitions between sleep stages are smooth, but in sleepwalkers, there's an incomplete awakening from deep NREM sleep. Parts of the brain responsible for movement and basic functions (like the motor cortex, cerebellum, and brainstem) activate, enabling complex behaviors, while higher-level areas for consciousness, decision-making, and memory (like the prefrontal cortex) remain offline or suppressed. This creates a dissociated state—awake enough to move but asleep enough to lack awareness. Factors like genetics, sleep deprivation, stress, fever, or certain medications can trigger it by destabilizing NREM sleep, leading to these arousals.
