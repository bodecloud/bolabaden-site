### **WormGPT and FraudGPT**
These models became infamous for their explicit focus on enabling illegal activities. Unlike mainstream LLMs, which have built-in safeguards to prevent the generation of harmful content, WormGPT and its counterparts were marketed as unrestricted versions of popular LLMs. They allowed users to generate malicious code, such as keyloggers, polymorphic malware (which can evade detection by constantly changing its structure), phishing emails, and other tools for cybercrime.[10]

- **FraudGPT** and similar models were designed for social engineering attacks, including generating phishing emails or impersonating individuals in online scams.[10]

### **Unregulated Outputs**
These models were controversial because they lacked the ethical restrictions found in more mainstream LLMs. While mainstream models like ChatGPT are programmed to refuse requests for harmful content (e.g., how to create malware or commit fraud), these alternative models were specifically designed to provide such information without restriction. This made them attractive to bad actors seeking tools for cybercrime, fraud, or even terrorism.

### **Community Backlash**
The release of these models sparked significant backlash from both the media and cybersecurity communities. For example, WormGPT was eventually shut down due to negative media coverage and public outcry over its potential for abuse. However, the existence of these models highlighted a growing concern about the proliferation of unregulated AI tools that could be used for malicious purposes.[10]

### **Conclusion**
The rise of unregulated LLMs like WormGPT and FraudGPT in 2024 marked a troubling trend in AI development. These models were specifically designed to enable illegal activities by removing ethical safeguards, making them highly controversial. Their release raised significant concerns about the potential misuse of generative AI technologies and highlighted the need for stronger regulation and oversight in the development and deployment of LLMs.

Many mainstream LLMs like ChatGPT were exploited through **jailbreaking techniques**, which allowed users to bypass safety features and generate harmful content. Jailbreaking involved using specific prompts or sequences of commands to unlock restricted functionalities in these models. This could lead to the generation of illegal or unethical content, including instructions for creating weapons or malware. Jailbreaking became a significant issue in 2024 as it allowed users to manipulate even well-regulated models into producing dangerous outputs.[1][6]

### **3. Dark Web AI Tools**
Cybercriminals also experimented with AI on the dark web, using LLMs to automate tasks like phishing, malware development, and data theft. ### **4. Prompt Injection Attacks**
Another significant issue was the rise of **prompt injection attacks**, where malicious actors manipulated LLMs by embedding harmful instructions within prompts. These attacks could lead to unintended actions by the model, such as generating misinformation, hateful speech, or even executing unauthorized financial transactions. Prompt injections posed a serious threat because they exploited the inherent malleability of LLMs, making it difficult to detect and prevent harmful outputs.[2]

In 2024, several **malicious LLMs** gained notoriety on the dark web for their use in cybercrime and other illicit activities. These models were either purpose-built for malicious purposes or jailbroken versions of legitimate models, designed to bypass ethical restrictions. Below are some of the most popular LLMs used on the dark web:

### **1. WormGPT**
- **Base Model**: GPT-J (an open-source model similar to GPT-3)
- **Primary Use**: WormGPT was one of the first malicious LLMs to appear on the dark web in mid-2023. It was primarily used for **business email compromise (BEC)** attacks, phishing, and generating sophisticated malware. 
- **Key Features**: It allowed users to generate unlimited text without ethical constraints, making it a powerful tool for creating malicious code and phishing emails.
- **Subscription Model**: Available via subscription, with prices ranging from €100 per month to €550 annually.[1][2]

### **2. FraudGPT**
- **Base Model**: GPT-3-like technology
- **Primary Use**: FraudGPT was designed for a wide range of cybercriminal activities, including writing malicious code, creating phishing pages, generating undetectable malware, and finding security vulnerabilities.
- **Key Features**: It was marketed as an all-in-one tool for cybercrime, with no ethical boundaries and capabilities for quick, high-volume phishing attacks.
- **Subscription Model**: Priced between $200 per month and $1,700 annually.[2][4]

### **3. XXXGPT**
- **Primary Use**: XXXGPT was designed to deploy botnets, remote access Trojans (RATs), cryptostealers, and other types of malware. It also included tools for ATM malware kits and infostealers.
- **Key Features**: The developers claimed to have a team of experts backing the tool, making it a customizable solution for various cybercriminal projects.[1]

### **4. PoisonGPT**
- **Base Model**: GPT-J
- **Primary Use**: PoisonGPT was crafted to demonstrate how LLMs could be used to spread misinformation by injecting false information into generated content. This model was designed specifically to deceive users by providing incorrect facts that could go undetected by standard benchmarks.
- **Key Features**: PoisonGPT used advanced techniques like the ROME algorithm to edit models post-training and inject false statements.[1]

### **5. DarkBERT**
- **Primary Use**: DarkBERT was originally developed by researchers to help combat cybercrime but was later adapted by malicious actors for illegal purposes. Trained on data from the dark web, it was used to automate tasks like phishing and malware development.
- **Key Features**: DarkBERT had access to internet data and could be integrated with tools like Google Lens for more sophisticated operations.[2]

### **6. DarkBARD**
- **Primary Use**: A dark web version of Google’s Bard AI, DarkBARD was in development as of 2024. It aimed to provide similar functionalities as Bard but without any ethical restrictions or safety guardrails.
- **Key Features**: Like other dark LLMs, DarkBARD was designed for offensive purposes such as generating scam content and malware.[2]

### Key Trends in Malicious LLM Usage:
- These models are often sold via subscription services on dark web marketplaces or encrypted messaging platforms like Telegram.
- They are marketed as *unrestricted* versions of mainstream AI tools like ChatGPT or Bard, offering capabilities that go beyond what legitimate models can provide due to ethical constraints.
- Many of these models are based on older open-source technologies like GPT-J or GPT-3 but are still effective due to their lack of safety filters.

These malicious LLMs represent a growing trend in AI-powered cybercrime, where generative AI is being leveraged not just for automation but also for enhancing the sophistication of attacks.
