### **What is Historical Fiction?**
Historical fiction is a genre that places its story in a distinct historical setting. The time period is not just a backdrop but a crucial element of the narrative. While some stories may feature real historical figures or events, others may not. The genre often blends with others, such as romance, thriller, fantasy, or horror.

### **Word Count and Sub-genres**
Historical fiction tends to have longer word counts, ranging from 75,000 to 100,000 words. Some epic historical fiction can even reach up to 150,000 or 180,000 words. Sub-genres are often categorized by time periods (e.g., Ancient Egypt or Victorian London) but can also include hybrid genres like historical fantasy or historical romance.

### **Tips for Writing Historical Fiction**

#### **1. Know Your Setting**
It’s essential to have a deep understanding of the time period and setting in which your story takes place. If you’re not already familiar with the era, be prepared for extensive research. The setting is key to immersing readers in the story.

#### **2. Make Research-Informed Decisions**
Before making any major plot decisions, conduct thorough research. Historical details can significantly impact your story, and inaccuracies can derail your narrative. For example, the video mentions an author who included CPR in a scene set before it was invented—an error that required revision.

#### **3. Organize Your Research**
Keep track of your research in an organized manner, similar to how fantasy writers create world-building bibles. This will help you stay consistent and easily access important details as you write.

#### **4. Respect Historical Timelines**
You don’t have control over historical events—they happened when they happened. Ensure that your story aligns with actual historical timelines to avoid contradictions. Even small changes to character ages or backstories can require significant adjustments to maintain historical accuracy.

#### **5. Use Primary and Secondary Sources**
Primary sources (e.g., literature or personal accounts from the time) provide insight into what it was like to live during that period and offer rich details for your writing. Secondary sources (e.g., works by historians) provide factual information but may lack the lived experience conveyed by primary sources.

#### **6. Worldbuilding**
Historical fiction requires as much worldbuilding as genres like fantasy or science fiction—though the world already exists, you need to bring it alive on the page through immersive descriptions and sensory details.

#### **7. Check Your Word Choice**
Ensure that the language you use fits the time period. Tools like Google Ngrams can help you determine whether certain words or phrases were commonly used during your chosen era. Etymonline is another useful resource for checking when specific words were first recorded.

#### **8. Find an Interesting Point of Access**
When writing about real historical events or people, find a unique angle rather than simply retelling well-known stories. You might take creative liberties by filling in gaps in history or exploring rumors and mysteries surrounding famous figures.

#### **9. Pick a Consistent Level of Accuracy**
Decide how accurate you want your story to be and stick with it throughout the narrative. Some works aim for strict historical accuracy, while others take more creative liberties (e.g., TV shows like *Dickinson*). Whatever approach you choose, maintain consistency so readers know what to expect.

#### **10. Make Mundane Details Interesting**
In historical fiction, everyday activities—like doing laundry in medieval times—can be fascinating because they differ from modern life. These mundane details can enhance immersion and add depth to your worldbuilding.

#### **11. Outline (Optional but Recommended)**
While outlining isn’t mandatory, it’s highly recommended for historical fiction due to the fixed nature of historical timelines and events. Even if you prefer discovery writing (writing without an outline), creating at least a loose timeline can help prevent contradictions and save time during revisions.

#### **12. Show History Through Scenes**
Avoid long blocks of exposition summarizing historical facts; instead, show history through active scenes that engage readers with characters and events directly involved in their world.

#### **13. Dialogue**
The question of whether dialogue should reflect how people spoke at the time depends on accessibility for modern readers and consistency with your narrative voice:
- If replicating old speech patterns would make dialogue difficult to read (e.g., Old English), it’s better to simplify.
- Ensure that dialogue matches the tone of your narrative voice—whether it’s formal and timeless or modernized.
- Consistency between dialogue and narrative style is crucial for maintaining immersion.

In conclusion, writing historical fiction requires careful research, attention to detail, and consistency in both accuracy and creative liberties taken within the story's framework.
