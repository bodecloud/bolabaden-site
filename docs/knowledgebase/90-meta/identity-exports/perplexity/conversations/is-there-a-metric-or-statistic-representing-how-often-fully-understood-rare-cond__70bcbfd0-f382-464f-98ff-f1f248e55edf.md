# Is there a metric or statistic representing how often fully understood rare conditions are diagnosed correctly after death due to misdiagnosis vs while they are alive?

Source: [Perplexity thread](https://www.perplexity.ai/search/c2e7de1d-d3b4-49c5-a9fd-194b1814ad39)

## User

Is there a metric or statistic representing how often fully understood rare conditions are diagnosed correctly after death due to misdiagnosis vs while they are alive?

## Assistant

There does not appear to be a specific, widely recognized metric or statistic that directly compares the rate at which fully understood rare conditions are accurately diagnosed postmortem versus while the patient is alive. However, several studies and findings provide insight into diagnostic discrepancies and challenges that may relate to this question:

1. **Postmortem Diagnostic Discrepancies**: Studies have shown significant rates of discrepancies between clinical diagnoses made during life and findings from postmortem examinations. For example, one study found that postmortem findings were in complete agreement with predeath diagnoses in fewer than half of cases (45%), with major missed diagnoses present in 39% of cases. Conditions like myocardial infarction, carcinoma, and pulmonary embolism were among the most frequently missed diagnoses. While this study does not focus exclusively on rare diseases, it highlights the broader issue of diagnostic errors.[1][7]

2. **Rare Disease Misdiagnosis**: Rare diseases are particularly prone to being misdiagnosed due to their clinical heterogeneity and overlap with more common conditions. Surveys reveal that rare disease patients are often misdiagnosed multiple times before receiving an accurate diagnosis, with an average diagnostic delay of 4.7 years. Misdiagnoses can lead to delayed or inappropriate treatments and may contribute to undiagnosed or misdiagnosed deaths.[4][5][8]

3. **Impact of Postmortem Examinations**: Postmortem examinations remain a critical tool for identifying missed diagnoses, including rare conditions. In some cases, knowledge of postmortem findings could have altered treatment and potentially improved outcomes for patients if diagnosed earlier.[7][1]

4. **Exome Sequencing and Genetic Testing**: Advances in genetic testing, such as exome sequencing, have improved diagnostic accuracy for rare diseases. However, even with these tools, many patients remain undiagnosed or misdiagnosed during life. This underscores the potential for postmortem genetic analyses to identify rare conditions that were not diagnosed during life.[6]

While these findings collectively suggest that misdiagnosis of rare conditions during life is common and that postmortem examinations can reveal missed diagnoses, there is no single metric quantifying how often fully understood rare conditions are diagnosed only after death compared to during life. The lack of such a metric reflects the complexity of tracking diagnostic outcomes across diverse healthcare settings and the variability in performing postmortem examinations.
