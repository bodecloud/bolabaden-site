# Why do we only see one side of the moon?

Source: [Perplexity thread](https://www.perplexity.ai/search/c19cf957-35bc-4927-95dd-c2ab2908af67)

## User

Why do we only see one side of the moon?

## Assistant

We only see one side of the Moon because it is **tidally locked** to Earth. This means the Moon's rotation period matches its orbit around Earth, both taking about 27.3 days. As a result, the same hemisphere always faces Earth. However, due to a slight wobble called **libration**, we can observe about 59% of the Moon's surface over time. Tidal locking occurred because Earth's gravity gradually slowed the Moon's rotation until it synchronized with its orbit.[1][2][3]

## Assistant

The reason we don’t see the far side of the Moon, even with a small discrepancy, is due to the phenomenon of **tidal locking**. This process occurs because of the gravitational interaction between Earth and the Moon, which has caused the Moon's rotation and orbital periods to become synchronized over time.

Here’s why a discrepancy allowing us to see the far side doesn’t happen:

### 1. **Gravitational Forces and Tidal Locking**
Tidal locking happens when a smaller body (the Moon) orbits a larger one (Earth), and over millions of years, gravitational forces between them cause the smaller body to slow down its rotation. In the case of the Moon, Earth's gravity created tidal bulges on its surface when it was still rotating faster than it orbited. These bulges acted like a brake, gradually slowing down the Moon’s rotation. Eventually, the Moon’s rotation period matched its orbital period around Earth, reaching a stable state where one side always faces Earth.

### 2. **Stable Equilibrium**
Once tidal locking is achieved, it becomes a stable equilibrium. Any small discrepancies in rotation would be corrected by Earth's gravitational pull. If the Moon were to rotate slightly faster or slower than its current rate, Earth's gravity would exert torque on it, pulling it back into alignment with its orbit. This prevents any significant deviation from the current state where we only see one side.

### 3. **Libration: Slight Oscillations**
While we do not see the far side of the Moon directly, there is a phenomenon called **libration** that allows us to see slightly more than just 50% of its surface over time. Libration occurs because:
   - The Moon’s orbit around Earth is not perfectly circular but elliptical.
   - The Moon's axis is slightly tilted.
   - The Moon’s speed varies as it moves along this elliptical orbit.

These factors cause small oscillations in the Moon’s orientation relative to Earth, allowing us to see about **59%** of its surface from Earth over time. However, this still doesn’t reveal the far side entirely because these oscillations are limited.

### 4. **Why No Large Discrepancy?**
Any significant discrepancy in rotation would require external forces (such as another large celestial body exerting influence) or internal changes (like an alteration in mass distribution within the Moon). Since no such forces are acting on the Moon in a way that would disrupt its tidal lock with Earth, we continue to see only one hemisphere.

In summary, tidal locking has caused the Moon's rotation to perfectly match its orbit around Earth. While libration allows us to glimpse slightly more than half of its surface, there’s no large discrepancy in rotation because Earth's gravity keeps it tightly synchronized.
